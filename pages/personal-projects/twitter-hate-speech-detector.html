The Twitter Hate Speech Detector is a fine-tuned BERT model trained to classify racist and hateful tweets, achieving 82% validation accuracy across a dataset of over 10,000 tweets. The model was built in PyTorch and is published on Hugging Face at shalinialisha/bert-racist-tweets. See also Technical Skills.

Motivation

Automated hate speech detection is an active area of NLP research with direct implications for platform safety and content moderation. This project was built to develop hands-on experience with transformer-based classification and to explore the practical challenges of building a reliable hate speech classifier — including dataset imbalance, label ambiguity, and the limitations of accuracy as a sole evaluation metric.

Model and Training

The detector fine-tunes BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) on a labeled dataset of tweets. BERT's pre-training on large text corpora gives it strong contextual representations of language, which fine-tuning adapts to the specific task of hate speech classification. The training pipeline was built in PyTorch.

The model achieved 82% validation accuracy across a dataset of more than 10,000 tweets. Cross-validation was used to ensure the accuracy estimate was robust and not dependent on a particular train/test split.

Dataset

The model was trained on a dataset of over 10,000 tweets labeled for hate speech content, with a focus on racist language. Dataset curation involved cleaning raw tweet data, handling class imbalance, and applying consistent labeling criteria — standard challenges in content moderation research datasets.

Published Model

The fine-tuned model is published on Hugging Face at shalinialisha/bert-racist-tweets, making it available for inspection, download, and further research. Publishing the model reflects a commitment to open, reproducible work in a domain where transparency about model behavior matters.

Limitations and Considerations

Like all hate speech classifiers, this model has limitations. Accuracy on a held-out test set does not fully capture real-world performance — context, irony, reclaimed language, and evolving slang can all challenge classifiers trained on static datasets. The model is a research artifact, not a production moderation system, and the project was undertaken in part to build understanding of exactly these limitations.
