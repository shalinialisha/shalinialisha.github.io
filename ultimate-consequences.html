The Ultimate Consequences Project is a human rights data research initiative documenting conflict-related political deaths in Bolivia since 1982. As Research Assistant and Data Visualization Coder, Shalini compiled and analyzed a database of over 1,000 fatalities, implemented rigorous data validation for sensitive mortality data, and built interactive web dashboards to communicate findings to policy stakeholders. The project's most significant finding was surfacing critical gaps in the documentation of indigenous deaths. It connects to Shalini's broader engagement with political science and her use of technical skills in service of human rights.

Background

Bolivia has experienced significant political conflict since its return to democracy in 1982, including episodes of civil unrest, government repression, and protest-related violence. The Ultimate Consequences Project systematically documents the human cost of this conflict, with implications for accountability, historical memory, and policy reform.

Database Construction

The project involved compiling and analyzing a comprehensive database of over 1,000 deaths related to political conflict in Bolivia since 1982. Each record required careful coding across multiple dimensions: geographic location, cause of death, incident type, and victim demographics. Rigorous data cleaning, coding, and validation methods were implemented to ensure the accuracy and reliability of this sensitive mortality data.

The Indigenous Documentation Gap

A significant research finding was the identification of systematic gaps in the documentation of deaths among indigenous communities. This gap reflects broader patterns of structural invisibility — where marginalized populations are underrepresented not only in political life but also in the data systems meant to account for violence against them. Communicating this finding was a central output of the project.

Methodology

Analysis was conducted in R, with data validation pipelines designed to catch inconsistencies in a dataset assembled from heterogeneous source documents. Summary descriptive tables captured fatality rates by geographic location, cause of death, and incident type. The analytical approach combined quantitative pattern analysis with careful attention to the qualitative context of each event.

Interactive Visualizations

Dynamic visualizations were created using R and the Crosstalk package, enabling interactive exploration of the database. These visualizations were deployed as web dashboards — accessible to policy stakeholders and advocates who needed to explore the data without running R themselves. The interactive format allowed users to filter by geography, cause, and time period, surfacing patterns that static charts could not convey.

Policy Impact

Findings were communicated directly to policy stakeholders through the interactive dashboards, with the goal of informing policy discussions and reform efforts around human rights documentation. Shalini collaborated with stakeholders to incorporate feedback and ensure findings were actionable rather than merely descriptive.

Connection to Research Interests

This project planted a seed for Shalini's later focus on algorithmic systems and marginalized communities: the challenge of what gets counted — and what doesn't — is as relevant to data pipelines as it is to human rights databases. See Research Interests.
